Nikon Coolpix S7000 Review
A slim and lightweight long zoom camera which wont pinch your pockets
By Ershad Kaleebullah on 2015-08-17
Key Features
  • 20x optical zoom
  • 99 focus points
  • 3-inch display
  • 1/2.3-inch CMOS sensor
What we like
  • Slim design
  • Lightweight and comfortable to use
  • Fast shooting speeds
  • Good ISO performance
What we don't like
  • Favours slow shutter speeds in Auto mode
  • Battery life could have been better
  • Display is washed out under bright sunlight
  • Not suited for shooting videos

FAQ
B8Question 1
We understand that buying a camera is a daunting task and manufacturers don’t make it any easier by throwing a deluge of technical specifications at you. Understanding these specifications are important but what is even more important is answering two questions - why do you need a camera, and what you intend to shoot with the camera? Once you have answered those questions, the specifications can follow
Design and ergonomics
8

When we picked up the Coolpix S7000 for the very first time, we were really surprised by how light it was. At 161g, the Coolpix S7000 is extremely comfortable for one handed usage. The great ergonomics is aided by its compact dimensions of 99.5x60.0x27.4mm. We could easily slip it into our pocket and fish it out the moment we wanted to shoot a picture. 

We got the golden coloured variant of the Coolpix S7000 for review but it is also available in pink, silver and black colours. The golden variant has has a glossy finish and the shiny exterior looks rather attractive. Furthermore, the plastic build of camera feels pretty solid and should be able to withstand some amount of rough usage.

The front of the camera is mostly dominated by the 25–500mm f3.4-6.5 lens. The flash, and two microphones for stereo audio recording in video is also present on the front. The top portion of the camera houses the power button, which is flush with the edge and as a result the tactile feedback doesn’t feel good. The shutter button is also carved on the top edge and is surrounded by the zoom ring. The shutter button has decent amount of travel and also makes a distinctive click sound. Besides the shutter button lies the mode dial ring. 

The right edge of the camera has a flap that conceals the Micro-HDMI port and the Micro-USB port. We like the fact that Nikon has decided to go with a Micro-USB port for charging and data transfers which means you can charge the Coolpix S7000 using your smartphone charger as well, that is if it also uses the Micro-USB standard. Also on the right edge, are present two holes for the lanyard loop. The tripod socket and the battery/microSD card compartment are on the bottom edge.

The rear side of the camera is mostly dominated by the large 3-inch TFT LCD. The display outputs image at a resolution of 460k dots. It is bright and displays fairly accurate colours although under bright sunlight it gets washed out. We really liked the addition of a recessed thumb grip on the rear. Sitting besides this grip, one can find the movie record button. There is a circular d-pad with the ‘ok’ button bang in the center. The other buttons include playback, connectivity, menu and delete.

Performance
7

The major USP of the S7000 is its 20x optical zoom lens. This means that you can effectively go up to 500mm on the telephoto end. While this is a standout feature for a camera of this size and weight, there are issues. It was almost impossible for us to capture a shake-free image at full zoom, despite the in-build vibration reduction. You will need a tripod for stable shots at maximum zoom. 

The quality of images captured in day light were good, if not great. The colours were vibrant and the different gradients in a particular colour were distinguishable; take a look at the picture above to gauge for yourself. One thing we’ve noticed in budget cameras is chromatic aberration or purple fringing around the edges of the picture but it wasn’t the case with the Coolpix S7000, which is good. The camera also manages to expose all the areas really well and we didn’t face any issues with under-exposure. Furthermore, the macro performance of the Coolpix S7000 is not great when compared to cameras like the Canon Ixus 265 HS which can go as close as 1cm of the subject. 

In low-light conditions, the S7000 struggles to focus on the subject. Also, the details were missing. We also noticed some purple fringing. However, the noise levels were pretty low as the camera sticks to ISO 800, which is a sweet spot for it - head over to the next section to find out why. 

ISO Quality Test

The Coolpix S7000 operates in the ISO range of ISO 125 to ISO 6400. Before running our ISO quality test, we decided to keep our expectations in check considering the S7000 is after all an entry-level compact camera. And, we were pleasantly surprised by the results. The Coolpix S7000 managed to hold its own till ISO 800 and the noise levels were pretty low. Beyond ISO 1600, the images are almost unusable when blown up. 



Shooting Speeds

The Coolpix S7000 is one of the fastest shooters in this price range. In our testing the Coolpix S7000 had a consistent start-up time of 0.99 seconds, which is exactly what Nikon claims. Additionally if the light is good, the Coolpix S7000 can also focus fairly quickly and take a shot in an instant. However, the camera doesn’t allow you to control the shutter speed in any mode and generally tends to favour slower shutter speeds, despite having a fast shutter speed of 1/4000, for most of the shots. Using the Sports mode might help if you still want to persist, but be forewarned that the results are still pretty average. We’d not recommend using the Coolpix S7000 for shooting fast moving subjects. 

Video

The S7000 can shoot Full-HD 1080p videos at 25fps. We captured a few videos and found that they skipped frames a few times. Moreover, the microphones capture really poor quality audio; we noticed a lot of interference in sound. Zooming in is a smooth process and the vibration reduction comes in really handy. That said, the S7000 finds it difficult to retain focus once zoomed into the subject. 

Nikon bundles a 700mAh EN-EL19 battery with the camera and it has a CIPA rating of approximately 210 shots and 40 minutes of video shooting. In our testing we noticed that the claim was fairly accurate but even then we feel that Nikon could have done better on the battery life front. If you are planning to take the Coolpix S7000 out on a trip you will definitely need a spare battery. Thankfully, replacements are easily available.

 

Special features
6

As is necessary, considering today’s technology landscape, Nikon adds Wi-Fi and NFC capabilities to the Coolpix S7000. You need to download Nikon’s Wireless Mobile Utility app on you phone and link your camera to the smartphone using Wi-Fi. Using the app, you can remotely control the camera to capture images and view ones that are already present on the camera. Remotely controlling the camera is a minimal process that only allows you to capture the images and all the other settings can only be accessed on the camera. 

Conclusion

After spending more than a week with the camera, we are convinced that Nikon has a winner in the Coolpix S7000. It is slim, weighs no more than any smartphone, has a long zoom lens, and can take good, if not great, images. Having said that, there are a few drawbacks like subpar video performance, average battery life, and mediocre performance in low light. All in all, at a retail price of approximately Rs. 10,000, the Coolpix S7000 is a good buy for anyone who wants a stylish shooter which can fit in your pocket and has a long zoom lens. If you want better looking images the Sony Cybershot DSC-WX80 and Canon Ixus 265 HS are better bets.
